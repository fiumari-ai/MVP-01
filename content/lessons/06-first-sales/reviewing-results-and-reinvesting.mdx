# Reviewing Results and Reinvesting

**Objective:** Analyze your first cycle and make a data-informed decision about your next move.

Your first inventory batch is sold or nearly sold. This is the moment most operators skip — they reinvest immediately without stopping to understand what actually happened. A 20-minute review session after your first cycle gives you the data to operate more effectively on the next one.

## The First-Cycle Review

Pull the following numbers from Seller Central:

1. **Total units sold**
2. **Gross revenue** (total received from Amazon before fees)
3. **Amazon fees paid** (referral fees + FBA fees, found in Payments > Transaction View)
4. **Product cost** (total paid to supplier + freight)
5. **Net profit** = Gross Revenue − Amazon Fees − Product Cost

Calculate your **actual margin** = Net Profit ÷ Gross Revenue × 100

Compare this to your pre-launch estimate. If your actual margin is within 5% of your projection, your research process was accurate. If it is significantly lower, identify why:
- Were Amazon's fees higher than estimated? (Re-check with the FBA Revenue Calculator using actual fees)
- Did you need to reprice lower than planned to compete?
- Were there unexpected costs (extra FBA prep fees, damaged units, returns)?

## The Three Decisions After Your First Cycle

**Decision 1 — Reorder (the product is working)**
Margin is on target, sell-through was consistent, Buy Box was competitive. Reorder at 1.5–2x your original quantity. The product is validated. Scale your investment.

**Decision 2 — Adjust and reorder (the product is working with modifications)**
Margin is acceptable but lower than target. Identify the cause. If it is a pricing issue, test a slightly higher price on the next batch. If it is a cost issue, negotiate with the supplier on a larger order. Reorder a similar quantity while you optimize.

**Decision 3 — Move on (the product is not worth continuing)**
Margin is below 15%, or the product did not sell through in a reasonable time. Do not reinvest. Liquidate remaining inventory if needed (reduce price until it clears) and redirect capital to a better product. This is not failure — this is exactly how the research and test loop is supposed to work.

## Reinvestment Strategy

As your first product generates profit, reinvest that profit into your next product. This is how the operation grows without requiring external capital.

**Simple reinvestment model:**
- Product 1 generates $400 profit on first cycle
- Reinvest $400 as additional capital for Product 2's first order
- Now you are operating two products simultaneously
- Each cycle, profit from both products funds a third

By cycle 3–4, you have multiple products generating consistent revenue. The operation compounds.

## One Metric to Track Above All Others

**Days to Sell-Through:** How many days did it take to sell your entire inventory batch?

If you sold 30 units in 20 days, your sell-through rate is 1.5 units/day. At that rate, you should reorder when you have 10–15 days of stock remaining (to account for supplier and shipping lead time).

Tracking sell-through rate prevents stockouts (which tank your BSR and lose your Buy Box) and overstocking (which ties up capital and incurs FBA storage fees).

---

**Takeaway:** Every first cycle is a data collection exercise. Review the numbers honestly, make one of three decisions (reorder, adjust, move on), and reinvest profit into the next product. The loop is how the operation grows.
