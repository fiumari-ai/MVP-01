# Monitoring Your First Listing

**Objective:** Know what to watch, how often to check it, and how to respond to what you see.

Your listing is live. Now you wait — but not passively. The first 30 days generate data that tells you whether the product is working and what adjustments to make. This lesson gives you the monitoring framework.

## What to Check (and How Often)

**Daily (takes 3–5 minutes):**
- Are orders coming in? (Seller Central > Orders > Manage Orders)
- Is your listing still in the Buy Box? (Search your product keyword and see if your listing appears with the Add to Cart button)
- Any account health alerts? (Performance > Account Health dashboard)

**Every 3–5 Days:**
- What is your current Buy Box percentage? (Reports > Business Reports > Detail Page Sales and Traffic)
- Has a competitor changed their price, affecting your position?
- Is your listing suppressed or have quality alerts? (Inventory > Manage Inventory)

**Weekly:**
- Unit velocity: how many units are you selling per day/week?
- Revenue and profit: does it match your pre-launch calculation?
- Inventory level: at current velocity, when will you run out? Do you need to reorder?

## The Three Scenarios After Week 1

**Scenario A — It's selling on pace:**
Products are moving, Buy Box share is solid, margin is tracking. Action: monitor, plan your reorder timing. If you have 30 units left and you are selling 5/week, order within the next 2 weeks to avoid stocking out.

**Scenario B — Selling, but slowly:**
Units are moving, but slower than BSR data suggested. Check your Buy Box share. If you are not getting Buy Box, your price may be too high relative to competitors. Reprice within your margin floor and see if velocity improves.

**Scenario C — No sales:**
If zero units have sold after 7–10 days, investigate:
1. Are you in the Buy Box? Search your product manually — does your listing appear as the primary buying option?
2. Is your listing active and not suppressed?
3. Is your price competitive vs. current FBA offers?
4. Is there a new competitor who undercut the market significantly?

Most zero-sales situations resolve when pricing is corrected. Occasionally the product research was off — BSR had spiked temporarily and you entered during a lull. If no sales after 2 weeks with competitive pricing, the product may need to be repriced more aggressively, or abandoned after this inventory cycle.

## Responding to Your First Review

Your first customer review — positive or negative — is important data.

**Positive review:** Validation that the product is as described and customers are satisfied. Do nothing except feel good about the sourcing decision.

**Negative review (complaint about the product):** This is feedback from the market. If the product has a recurring defect or does not match what customers expect, address it. This might mean adjusting your listing description, contacting the supplier, or discontinuing the product after this batch.

**Negative review (complaint about seller):** If you receive a seller-specific complaint (late shipping, wrong item), address it directly by resolving the customer's issue. Do not ask them to change the review — that violates Amazon's policies.

---

**Takeaway:** Check your listing daily for the first two weeks. Identify which scenario you are in and take one specific action. Most first-listing issues are pricing or Buy Box problems, not product problems.
