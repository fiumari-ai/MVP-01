# The 5-Point Product Filter

**Objective:** Apply a systematic five-question filter to evaluate any product before committing capital or time.

Most new operators lose money not because they cannot find products — they lose because they move on products that look good at a glance but fail on one or two critical dimensions. This filter is designed to catch those failures before they happen.

Run every product you are considering through all five points. If it fails any one of them, move on.

## Point 1 — Is There Consistent Demand?

Check the BSR. Is it under 80,000 in its primary category? Is the BSR history consistent, or does it spike and crash? A product with a volatile BSR that is only occasionally low is not consistently selling — it is selling in bursts, which makes inventory timing difficult.

**Pass:** BSR under 80,000, stable for at least 3 months.
**Fail:** BSR over 100,000, or BSR that spikes wildly.

## Point 2 — Is There Margin After Fees?

Amazon charges a referral fee (typically 8–15% depending on category), and if you are using FBA, a fulfillment fee based on item size and weight. Before you calculate margin, you need to know your total cost per unit after all fees.

Use Amazon's FBA Revenue Calculator (free tool in Seller Central) to enter your expected sale price and your cost per unit. It will calculate your net profit per unit after Amazon's fees.

**Target:** At minimum 25% net margin after all fees and product cost. Better is 30–40%.
**Fail:** Net margin under 20%, or a margin that disappears entirely if the price drops by $2.

## Point 3 — Can You Compete for the Buy Box?

The Buy Box is the "Add to Cart" button on an Amazon product page. If multiple sellers are listing the same product, Amazon rotates the Buy Box among sellers who meet certain criteria — primarily competitive pricing and good seller metrics. If you are not in the Buy Box rotation, you are essentially invisible.

**What to check:** How many FBA sellers are currently listing this product? If there are 50 FBA sellers competing aggressively, it will be hard to win Buy Box share as a new seller. If there are 3–10 sellers, that is a manageable competitive set.

**Pass:** Under 10–15 competing FBA sellers, no dominant brand monopolizing the listing.
**Fail:** 30+ competing sellers, or the listing is controlled by the brand itself (who will almost always win the Buy Box).

## Point 4 — Is the Price Stable?

Pull the Keepa graph. Look at the price history for 6–12 months. Is the selling price holding? Or is it in a long-term decline?

A product with a stable price for a year is a product that is not being commoditized. A product with a price that has dropped 30% in 6 months is one you are late to and will have declining margins.

**Pass:** Price stable or slightly trending up over 6 months.
**Fail:** Price declining month over month.

## Point 5 — Can You Source It?

This is the practical gate. Even if a product passes the first four filters, you need to be able to source it at a price that supports your margin target. A product that is only available from a supplier with a $5,000 minimum order is not accessible at the early stage.

For your first product, you want a supplier who will accept a first order of $200–$500. We cover supplier sourcing in the next module — but apply this filter here conceptually. If you already know a supplier category you can access, prioritize products that fit that category.

**Pass:** Product is available from accessible wholesale sources at a price supporting 30%+ margin.
**Fail:** Product is only available direct from brand, at prohibitive minimums, or margin does not work at available prices.

---

**Takeaway:** Five questions. Demand, margin, competition, price stability, sourceable. A product that passes all five is worth pursuing. One failure is enough to move on.
