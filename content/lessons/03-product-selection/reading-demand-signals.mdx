# Reading Demand Signals

**Objective:** Understand the data signals on Amazon that tell you whether a product is worth selling.

Product selection is the most important skill in this model. The wrong product means slow sales, trapped inventory, and wasted capital. The right product means predictable turnover, healthy margin, and a cycle you can repeat. This lesson teaches you how to read the signals Amazon already shows you — for free.

## Best Seller Rank (BSR)

Every product listed on Amazon is assigned a Best Seller Rank in its category. A lower number means it sells more. BSR #1 in a category sells the most units. BSR #500,000 sells very little.

**What to look for:** In most categories, products with a BSR under 100,000 sell at least a few units per month. Products under 50,000 sell consistently. Products under 10,000 are high-velocity sellers. For a reseller entering a category for the first time, targeting the 10,000–80,000 BSR range is a good starting point — enough demand to move inventory, not so competitive that you are fighting major brands for every sale.

BSR fluctuates daily. A product at BSR 15,000 today might be at 25,000 tomorrow if a competitor ran a sale. Look at the BSR history, not just the current number.

## Review Count and Velocity

The number of reviews a product has tells you two things: how established it is, and how competitive the listing is.

- **High review count (500+):** The product has been selling for a while. Established demand. But also more competition and harder to win the Buy Box.
- **Low review count (under 50):** Newer listing. May indicate an emerging product — or a product that is not selling.
- **Review velocity:** If a product is accumulating new reviews every week, it is actively selling. If reviews stopped 6 months ago, the product may have stalled.

For resellers, you are not creating a new product — you are joining an existing listing. A listing with 100–500 reviews, still receiving new reviews regularly, with a healthy BSR is the target.

## Price History

Price history tells you whether a product's price is stable, declining, or artificially inflated. Tools like Keepa (a free browser extension) show you a graph of price changes over time.

**What to look for:**
- **Stable price:** A product holding its price over 6–12 months signals a healthy, non-commoditized market. This is what you want.
- **Declining price:** If the price has been dropping for months, margin compression is happening. Other sellers are competing down. Avoid entering a race to the bottom.
- **Temporary spikes:** Sometimes prices spike because a seller ran out of stock. If you source during a spike, you may be buying high and listing into a normalized (lower) market. Check the average, not the peak.

## Search Volume

Search volume tells you how many people are looking for the type of product you are researching. Amazon's own search suggest (what auto-completes when you type in the search bar) is a free signal — if your product's keyword auto-completes quickly, it is being searched frequently.

More precise data is available through tools like Helium 10 (free tier) or AMZScout. For early-stage research, Amazon's own search bar and BSR data are enough to identify viable products.

---

**Takeaway:** BSR tells you how much a product sells. Reviews tell you how established it is. Price history tells you whether margin is sustainable. These three signals are enough to filter most bad product ideas before you spend a dollar.
